A week with Melbourne...

Before we faced lockdown, what did a typical week look like for Melbournians?

Started in 2009, the Pedestrian Counting System is an array of sensors placed in strategic locations around Melbourne to track pedestrian movement of every hour of every day. Its open-source availability allows for visualisation both before and during the current COVID-19 lockdown.

Activity over Time

The map below is a visualisation of Melbourne pedestrian activity, from August 5th to August 11th, 2019. The circles mark the locations of various pedestrian sensors that provide an hourly count of pedestrians seen at that location. Click on any of the scatterplot squares below to view activity at that point in time.

Here are a few insights as to what happens during a standard week in Melbourne:

Weekday Rush Hours

On weekdays at 8am, there are large spikes in pedestrian movement around Southern Cross and Flinders Street Stations. This is most likely people making the commute to work via train and public transport. We see a spike in these transit areas again at 5pm, when people are starting to leave work and head home.

On weekends, we don't see a rush hour. Saturday and Sunday activity starts to peak around midday to early afternoon.

Busy Midday

Throughout the week, The midday period is one of the busiest days. It's possible that people are venturing out to grab lunch, whether for a quick bite to eat during the weekday, or for planned meetups on the weekend.

One thing to note is that the increase in activity happens throughout the city. This is evident by flicking between 11am and 12pm on any given day.

Friday, Weekend Nightlife

The city is still active late at night, especially on Friday and Saturday nights. People are either finished with their work week, or are in the city for leisure reasons.

Also note the large spike on Friday 6pm at Bourke Street Bridge. This spike is most likely due to a sports game happening at the nearby Docklands Stadium drawing audiences.

Activity by Sensor

We can also group pedestrian activity by which sensor recorded the movement. On the left hand side is the proportion of weekday and weekend pedestrian counts. On the right hand side is the total amount of pedestrians seen by a given sensor.

Typically, a sensor that sees an even amount of weekend and weekday activity will have a 30:70 split between the two. Locations with a higher weekend to weekday ratio typically serve more as a recreational location, such as Birrarung Marr or Chinatown. Conversely, places with a lower weekend to weekday ratio may serve more of a commercial or transit need, such as Southern Cross Station.

...And a week without.

In January 2020, the first confirmed case of the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) in Australia was identified. Over the coming months, borders would close, businesses would shut, and Victoria as a whole would come to a halt. There was a brief respite at the start of 2021, only to be plunged back into another lockdown halfway through the year.

As of the 19th of October, 2021, the Victorian government is poised to re-open metropolitan Melbourne, taking the first steps in restarting the city. But what was the city like without tourism, nightlife, and people?

In Conclusion

Melbourne isn't the same city as it was two years ago, and it's very unlikely that we'll be getting the hustle and bustle we've been missing so dearly in the city.

When restrictions do open up, there is simply less reason to go back. Many workers have acclimatised to working from home, and many office-type businesses have moved to a remote working system. There is still the hope for the retail and recreation spaces, however many retailers have closed down since the start of lockdown.

One of the biggest influencing factors of activity is tourism, both domestic and international. With borders still closed, we won't be able to reach the same peaks of activty we've experienced earlier.

As the Pedestrian Counting System keeps collecting data for every hour of every day, we can continue to see the bigger picture of how COVID-19 has affected Melbourne.
